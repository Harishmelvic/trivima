# From Photograph to Navigable 3D World
## The Complete Trivima Pipeline

---

# Chapter 1: The Complete Vision

## 1.1 What This System Does

A user takes a single photograph of any environment — a room, a street, a landscape. Within two seconds, the system produces a navigable 3D world the user can walk through. The visible portion is photorealistically accurate, reconstructed directly from the photograph. The invisible portions — behind the camera, the backs of objects, the floor under furniture — are generated by AI. Empty spaces can be automatically furnished with style-matched furniture. Physics emerge from the cell grid's density field — the user cannot walk through walls because cells encode solidity, not because a physics engine calculates forces.

The rendering uses differential-integral cells with AI texturing, producing photorealistic output at 30-60 frames per second. The experience feels like walking through a photograph that has been brought to life in three dimensions.

## 1.2 The Eight-Stage Pipeline

Stage 1 — Perception: Extract 3D geometry, depth, and semantic labels from the photograph.
Stage 2 — Cell Grid Construction: Convert the 3D point cloud into differential-integral cells with gradients and integrals.
Stage 3 — Room Shell Extension: Geometrically extend the room's walls, floor, and ceiling to complete the architectural structure.
Stage 4 — AI Texturing: Generate photorealistic lighting and texture for all cells, including extended surfaces.
Stage 5 — Auto-Furnishing: Detect functional gaps and place style-matched furniture from a database.
Stage 6 — Validation: Check conservation laws (energy, mass, shadow direction) and apply corrections.
Stage 7 — Navigation: Enable walking through the world using cell-based collision and floor following.
Stage 8 — Rendering: Draw the cell grid at adaptive resolution with AI texturing enhancement at 30-60+ FPS.

# Chapter 2: Stage 1 — Perception

## 2.1 The Perception Models

Depth Pro (Apple) processes the image and produces a metric depth map — distance in meters from the camera to every pixel. Its multi-scale Vision Transformer processes the image at multiple resolutions simultaneously, capturing global room structure and sharp object boundaries. It estimates the camera's focal length from image content, enabling metric depth without camera metadata. Processing time: 0.3 seconds.

SAM 3 (Meta) segments the image into semantic regions — identifying every object, surface, and architectural feature. Its Promptable Concept Segmentation handles 270,000+ concepts with text-based detection, producing pixel-perfect masks with category labels and confidence scores. Processing time: 0.2 seconds.

DUSt3R (Naver Labs) is optional for single-image input. When the user provides multiple images, DUSt3R establishes cross-view correspondences and produces geometrically consistent point maps. For single-image input, bilateral depth smoothing on the Depth Pro output compensates for the missing geometric consistency.

## 2.2 Preprocessing

Bilateral depth smoothing: applied to the Depth Pro output before backprojection. Spatial sigma 3-4 pixels, guided by the RGB image. Smooths per-pixel depth noise (reducing gradient noise by 40-65%) while preserving sharp depth transitions at object boundaries. Processing time: 5 milliseconds. This step is essential for the cell architecture — without it, gradients computed by finite differences are dominated by noise rather than real spatial variation.

Scale calibration: the system automatically detects objects of known size — interior doors (approximately 200×80 centimeters), standard furniture heights, floor tiles. The scale correction factor reduces systematic depth error from 0.5-2% to below 0.3%. Processing time: 50 milliseconds.

Failure mode detection: SAM 3 labels trigger material-specific corrections. Mirror cells: forced to density 1.0, confidence 0.1 (prevents phantom room behind mirror). Glass cells: forced to density 1.0, confidence 0.2 (makes invisible glass a solid barrier). Dark scenes: all cells receive low confidence. Sky pixels: excluded from the cell grid, rendered as skybox.

## 2.3 Perception Output

A labeled, metric, smoothed 3D point cloud where every point has position (X, Y, Z) in meters, color (R, G, B), semantic label, and confidence score. Approximately 500,000-2,000,000 points for a typical room photograph. Total perception time: approximately 1 second.

# Chapter 3: Stage 2 — Cell Grid Construction

## 3.1 Point-to-Cell Conversion

The 3D point cloud is binned into a sparse grid of 5-centimeter cubic cells. Each cell aggregates the points within its volume to compute its properties.

Density: the solidity of this cell (0.0 for air, 1.0 for solid wall). Computed from point count — cells with many points are dense, cells with no points are empty.

Albedo: the true surface color, inherited from the photo's pixel colors at the corresponding points.

Normal: the surface direction, computed from the cross product of local point cloud gradients or from the 5×5 Sobel kernel applied to the depth map during perception.

Semantic label: the majority-vote label from SAM 3 for all points within the cell.

Confidence: derived from depth smoothness (low noise → high confidence), point density (more points → higher confidence), and semantic penalty (glass/mirror → low confidence regardless).

## 3.2 Gradient and Integral Computation

After cells are populated, spatial derivatives and integrals are computed by finite differences between adjacent cells.

Density gradient: points from solid toward empty. A high gradient means a surface boundary passes through this cell. The gradient direction and magnitude enable sub-cell surface localization — approximately 5-15mm precision within a 5cm cell.

Albedo gradient: the texture grain direction. A wood surface's albedo gradient follows the grain. This IS the texture detail — no separate texture map needed.

Normal gradient: the surface curvature. This IS the bump detail — no separate bump map needed.

Integrals: density integral is total mass (density × cell volume). Albedo integral is total color energy. These are used for conservation validation and correct merging during LOD changes.

Neighbor summaries: each cell stores the type, density, normal, and light value of its six adjacent cells, enabling local queries without global search.

## 3.3 The SoA Layout

Cells are split into CellGeo (64 bytes, hot — read every frame for collision) and CellVisual (448 bytes, cold — read only during rendering). Stored in separate contiguous arrays for 8× better cache utilization during collision checks. Total: 512 bytes per cell.

For a typical room: approximately 80,000 cells, approximately 40 MB. Cell grid construction takes approximately 0.5 seconds. Navigation can begin immediately after this stage.

# Chapter 4: Stage 3 — Room Shell Extension

## 4.1 Geometric Approach

The room shell is extended geometrically, not by AI generation. Walls are planar and vertical, floors are planar and horizontal, ceilings are planar and horizontal. These geometric facts enable confident extension using RANSAC plane fitting on existing surface cells.

Each visible wall's plane equation is estimated from its surface cells' positions and normals. The floor and ceiling planes are similarly estimated. New cells are generated along these planes extending to the room's estimated boundaries.

Extension cells have correct geometry (position, normal matching the parent plane), density 1.0 (solid surface), basic albedo (extrapolated from the nearest observed cells on the same plane), and moderate confidence (0.5-0.7 — geometrically correct but visually approximate).

## 4.2 What the Extension Produces

After shell extension, the scene has a complete architectural envelope. The visible portions (from the photograph) have photorealistic detail from real pixel data. The extended portions have correct geometry but flat, uniform textures. This plainness is addressed in Stage 4 (AI texturing), which paints photorealistic appearance onto the flat extension cells.

The quality gradient is inherent and honest: the front of the room (from the photo) is photorealistic. The sides (30-60° beyond the photo edges) are high quality. The back (behind the camera) is plausible but AI-generated. The further from the original viewpoint, the more the system is generating rather than reproducing.

Shell extension runs in the background after navigation begins. Time: 10-15 seconds. The user can walk around the visible portion while extension completes.

# Chapter 5: Stage 4 — AI Texturing

## 5.1 What the AI Model Does

The AI texturing model takes the cell grid's raw render (flat-shaded cells with albedo colors, depth, normals, and semantic labels) and produces photorealistic output — correct lighting, shadows, material appearance, and surface detail.

The model is a Pix2PixHD-Lite GAN (approximately 25 million parameters) for real-time use (<10ms per frame on A100) or a StreamDiffusion + ControlNet pipeline for production quality (15-50ms per frame).

The AI model replaces all traditional lighting computation. No shadow maps, no global illumination, no physically-based rendering equations. The AI model has learned what rooms look like under various lighting conditions and paints the correct appearance directly.

## 5.2 Cell Write-Back

The AI model outputs a 2D photorealistic image. This image is mapped back to the 3D cell grid using the cell ID buffer — a buffer rendered alongside the color buffer where each pixel stores the index of the cell it corresponds to.

Each pixel's color is written to its corresponding cell's light value, weighted by the viewing angle (face-on pixels contribute more, grazing-angle pixels contribute less). The light gradient is then computed by finite differences between neighboring cells' light values.

## 5.3 Temporal Consistency

Per-cell temporal blending prevents flickering between frames. Instead of screen-space EMA (which causes ghosting), blending happens in 3D cell space — cells have stable positions, so there's no parallax misalignment. Alpha is based on view angle change: cells whose viewing angle changed significantly get updated; stable cells retain their previous lighting.

Only 10-30% of cells need re-lighting per frame during slow navigation. The light temporal derivative (∂L/∂t) stored in each cell indicates how fast its lighting is changing — cells where ∂L/∂t ≈ 0 are skipped.

## 5.4 Surface Texture Generation for Extended Cells

Extended cells from shell extension have flat colors. The AI texturing model handles these too — it generates appropriate wall textures, floor grain patterns, and ceiling appearance based on the observed cells' visual style. The transition between real (photo-derived) cells and generated (extension) cells is handled by the AI model's training, which includes scenes with mixed real and generated regions.

Known limitation in the prototype: a visible quality seam between photo-derived cells and extension cells. The AI texturing model reduces this but doesn't eliminate it. Full elimination requires a dedicated texture synthesis model trained specifically for seamless extension — a future upgrade.

# Chapter 6: Stage 5 — Auto-Furnishing

## 6.1 VLM-Driven Gap Detection

Qwen3-VL examines the cell grid's semantic content and identifies functional gaps. The analysis follows a chain of reasoning: classify the room type from observed objects ("this is a living room — I see a sofa and TV"), compare against expected furnishing ("a living room typically has side tables, floor lamp, rug — all missing"), and identify suitable positions ("the corner to the right of the sofa has 1.2 square meters of open floor").

The output is a prioritized list of suggested items, each with category, approximate dimensions, and a suggested placement region.

## 6.2 Furniture Retrieval and Style Adaptation

For each suggested item, the system searches a database of 3D furniture models. The search is conditioned on category, style (matching the room's detected aesthetic), and dimensions (fitting the available space).

Retrieved models are style-adapted: a neural style transfer adjusts the texture to match the room's color palette and material style. A chrome floor lamp in a warm wood room gets its chrome shifted toward brushed brass. The geometry is unchanged — only the visual appearance adapts.

## 6.3 Placement via Coordinate-Validation

Each retrieved object is converted to cells (voxelized at 5cm resolution) and placed using the coordinate-validation system. The five validation fields (surface support, collision, boundary, functional, aesthetic) evaluate candidate positions. The VLM re-ranks the top 20-50 candidates based on design intelligence.

Sequential placement: each placed object updates the cell grid and validation fields before the next object is placed. The sofa's cells become collision obstacles for the coffee table's placement. The VLM provides lookahead — checking that the sofa position leaves room for dependent pieces.

## 6.4 Placement Quality

The coordinate-validation system guarantees physics: no floating objects, no collisions, no out-of-bounds placement. The VLM provides judgment: style-appropriate items in design-reasonable positions. The combination produces rooms that are both physically valid and aesthetically considered.

Validation system accuracy: 5-10cm position precision from single-image input. Physics violation rate: below 1%. VLM aesthetic agreement with human preference: target 80%+.

# Chapter 7: Stage 6 — Conservation Validation

## 7.1 What Is Checked

Three conservation laws are verified after each significant change to the cell grid (AI texturing update, object placement, LOD change).

Energy conservation: the total reflected light across all visible cells must not exceed the total incoming light. The cell light integrals enable this check — sum the reflected energy (albedo × light × cell area) and compare against the emissive cells' output. Tolerance: 5% per cell, 1% scene-wide.

Mass conservation: the total density integral must remain constant. Cells are not created or destroyed during navigation — only the LOD representation changes. The integral-weighted merge ensures that parent cells correctly summarize their children's total mass. Tolerance: 0.1%.

Shadow direction: the light gradient in shadow-receiving cells should align with the vector from the cell to the nearest light source. This catches AI texturing errors where shadows point the wrong way. Tolerance: dot product > 0.5 for 70%+ of cells.

## 7.2 Corrections

Violations are corrected gradually over 3-5 frames (not instantly, which would cause visible popping). Corrections run asynchronously, one frame behind rendering, so they never affect frame rate. Typical corrections are small — scaling a cell's light value by 2-5% to satisfy energy conservation.

# Chapter 8: Stage 7 — Navigation

## 8.1 Density-Based Collision

The camera has a virtual body (approximately 0.4m wide, 1.7m tall). As the camera moves, the system looks up the cell at each boundary point of the capsule. If the cell type is Solid or Surface with density above the threshold, movement is blocked in that direction. Wall-sliding projects the movement onto the surface plane for natural-feeling collision.

Sub-cell precision: the density gradient within boundary cells indicates exactly where the surface is within the 5cm cell. The collision point is computed from the gradient, achieving approximately 5-15mm precision without sub-cell resolution. This is a cell lookup plus one gradient evaluation — approximately 0.001 milliseconds.

## 8.2 Floor Following

The camera's vertical position is determined by scanning downward from the camera position for the highest surface cell. The camera height is set to floor height plus 1.6 meters (eye height). The height is smoothed over 3-4 frames to prevent sudden jumps.

Slope handling: the floor cell's normal indicates the local surface angle. Steep slopes reduce movement speed. The normal gradient indicates if the slope is changing (approaching a hill crest or valley).

Terrain following works identically for non-room environments — the "floor" is whatever the highest surface cell is below the camera, whether that's a concrete floor, a grassy hillside, or a sandy beach.

## 8.3 World Boundaries

Cells exist only within the generated world. Beyond the last cell, the camera cannot proceed. A soft visual treatment (increasing fog near the boundary) indicates the edge of the generated space without an abrupt cutoff.

## 8.4 What Navigation Does Not Handle

Objects do not fall when unsupported. Removing a table leaves its contents floating. The density field updates (the table region becomes empty) but items on the table are unchanged. For a design visualization tool, this is acceptable.

Objects have no momentum or inertia. Moving a chair teleports it. Soft body deformation does not occur. The world is a frozen moment that can be navigated and rearranged but not physically simulated.

# Chapter 9: Stage 8 — Rendering

## 9.1 The Cell Renderer

The renderer draws all visible cells as instanced cubes via ModernGL. Each frame: determine visible cells by frustum culling (checking which cells fall within the camera's view), select resolution per cell based on distance (near cells subdivide via Taylor expansion, far cells merge via integral-weighted averaging), render with flat shading from albedo + normals, apply AI texturing enhancement, display.

The cell ID buffer is rendered alongside the color buffer, enabling AI texturing write-back and user interaction (clicking a position identifies which cell was clicked).

## 9.2 Adaptive Resolution

Near cells (within 3 meters): subdivide to 2cm resolution using Taylor expansion. Child values are predicted from parent gradients and second derivatives. Cost: approximately 0.01ms per cell. Single-image input supports 1 level of reliable subdivision (5cm → 2.5cm). Multi-image supports 3 levels (5cm → 0.6cm).

Mid-range cells (3-15 meters): keep at base 5cm resolution.

Far cells (beyond 15 meters): merge to 10-20cm resolution. Parent values computed from children's integrals — total energy and mass are conserved exactly during merge.

Cell budget cap: maximum 200,000 visible cells to maintain 60+ FPS. Distant cells rendered as points instead of cubes for additional performance.

## 9.3 Progressive Entry

The user does not wait for the complete world before navigating. Content appears progressively:

At 1.5 seconds: perception and cell grid construction complete. The visible portion of the room is navigable in flat-shaded mode. The user can walk around the portion captured in the photograph.

At 10-15 seconds: shell extension complete. Walls, floor, and ceiling extend beyond the photograph. Extended surfaces have basic color but not yet detailed AI texturing.

At 15-30 seconds: AI texturing applied to all visible cells. The room looks photorealistic from all angles. Object backsides are generated via SAM 3D.

At 30-60 seconds: auto-furnishing complete (if activated). Suggested furniture appears in empty spaces. The user can accept, reject, or swap items.

At no point does the user see a loading screen or black void during navigation. Unfinished areas are shown with a subtle visual treatment (slight softness, simplified lighting) that indicates refinement is in progress.

# Chapter 10: The VLM's Role

## 10.1 Where Qwen3-VL Operates

Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct serves as the system's design intelligence layer. It operates at four points, never in the per-frame render loop. The 8B model is validated on A40 48GB at 17.5GB fp16 — fits alongside the cell renderer with room to spare.

Environment classification (once per scene, ~2 seconds): determines room type, design style, and functional zones. Drives shell extension strategy, auto-population rules, and validation parameters.

Auto-furnishing planning (once per scene, ~3-5 seconds): identifies functional gaps, proposes object categories, determines placement sequence (anchors first, dependent pieces second, accents last).

Aesthetic re-ranking (per placement, ~200-500ms): examines top 20-50 validation candidates and re-ranks by design quality — style coherence, cultural appropriateness, visual relationships.

Quality verification (once per scene): checks the final rendered world for scale mismatches, style clashes, and spatial inconsistencies.

## 10.2 Native 3D Grounding

Qwen3-VL has native 3D grounding through Interleaved-MRoPE and DeepStack — it can output 3D bounding boxes and reason about spatial relationships without custom positional encoding modifications. 3D context from the cell grid (distances, surface types, object positions) is provided via structured prompts rather than injected into the model's internals.

After distillation training on spatial QA data (achieved 88% within-25% accuracy from synthetic data in 4.8 minutes of training at $0.50 cost), the VLM combines its native 3D understanding with quantitative spatial precision. The validation system still handles all physics-critical decisions.

# Chapter 11: Comparison with World Labs Marble

## 11.1 The Landscape

World Labs launched its World API in January 2026 — a public interface for generating explorable 3D worlds from text, images, and video. They have raised over $1 billion in funding at a reported $5 billion valuation, with backing from Autodesk ($200M), AMD, Nvidia, and Fidelity. Founded by Fei-Fei Li.

Both systems produce navigable 3D worlds from photographs. The approach is fundamentally different.

## 11.2 Architecture Comparison

| Factor | World Labs (Marble) | Trivima |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | End-to-end neural (large world model generates everything) | Hybrid (perception + cell grid + AI texturing + validation) |
| Representation | Gaussian splats + collider mesh | Differential-integral cells (SoA, 512B) |
| Physics | Generated collider mesh (approximate) | Cell density field (computed, validated, conservation-checked) |
| Precision | Not disclosed | 5-10cm measured, error-propagation analyzed |
| Validation | None documented | Energy, mass, shadow conservation checks |
| Confidence | None documented | Per-cell confidence from 4 signals |
| Failure mode handling | None documented | Glass, mirror, dark, sky, specular mitigations |
| Grounding | Generated worlds (impressive but fictional) | Real photograph data (accurate for the visible portion) |
| Funding | $1B+ | Self-funded |

## 11.3 Where Marble Is Ahead

Visual quality of generated content: Marble's massive generative model produces higher-fidelity generated surfaces. Trivima's AI texturing model is smaller and produces good but not state-of-the-art generated textures.

Scale of environments: Marble handles multi-room, outdoor, and fantastical environments natively. Trivima is designed for single-environment reconstruction with shell extension.

Polish and ecosystem: Marble has a web API, integrations with OpenArt and Autodesk, and a growing developer community. Trivima is a research prototype.

## 11.4 Where Trivima Is Ahead

Accuracy for the observed portion: Trivima's visible region is reconstructed from real photograph data — the cells ARE the photo's pixels positioned in 3D. Marble generates everything, including the parts that could be directly observed.

Physics correctness: Trivima's cell density field provides validated collision with sub-cell precision. Conservation checks catch energy and mass violations. Per-cell confidence indicates where the geometry is reliable versus uncertain. Marble's collider meshes are generated approximations without validation.

Transparency: every Trivima cell carries a confidence score. The quality gradient (photo-derived front vs AI-generated back) is explicitly tracked and communicable to users. Marble's worlds have uniform uncertainty — there's no way to know which parts are more reliable than others.

Controllability: the coordinate-validation system provides explicit, debuggable placement decisions with score breakdowns. The VLM's aesthetic judgments can be overridden by adjusting validation weights. Marble's placement decisions are opaque neural network outputs.

Cost: Trivima's full training costs are under $25. Marble's training costs are not disclosed but estimated in the millions.

## 11.5 The Complementary Path

The ideal long-term architecture might combine both approaches: Trivima's perception, cell grid, and validation pipeline for accuracy and control in the observed region, with a Marble-scale generative model for high-quality content generation in the unseen regions. The cell architecture is designed to be model-agnostic — a future generative model could produce cells directly, slotting into the existing grid, validation, and rendering pipeline.

## 12.3 Hardware Requirements

For inference and real-time navigation: a single GPU with 48GB+ memory handles the entire pipeline (perception models run sequentially, cell grid + renderer share memory with AI texturing model). The A40 48GB is sufficient. An RTX 4090 24GB works for navigation but may be tight when running perception models.

For VLM inference: Qwen3-VL-8B at fp16 uses 17.5GB. Fits alongside the cell renderer on a 48GB GPU with room to spare.

For training: all training (GAN + VLM distillation) runs on a single A40. No multi-GPU training required at current model sizes.

## 12.4 Quality Expectations

From the original camera angle: target 75-90% visual match (SSIM) to the original photograph. The cell grid reconstructs the photo's pixels in 3D — from the same viewpoint, it should closely match the photo. Actual SSIM will be measured once the AI texturing GAN is trained.

From 30° off the original angle: target 60-75% visual match. Some surfaces visible from the new angle weren't in the photo — those are AI-generated.

From behind (fully generated): visually plausible but not matching the actual room. Architecturally correct geometry with AI-generated textures. No SSIM target — this is generation, not reconstruction.

Navigation: 60+ FPS on A40 with AI texturing active. No wall clipping, no floor falling. Cell-based collision with sub-cell gradient precision.

Auto-furnished items: physically valid (guaranteed by validation system), stylistically appropriate (80%+ human agreement target from VLM re-ranking).
